About the Project

Extracurricular activities are an integral part of undergraduate experience. Currently, at the University of Toronto, students do not have a central medium to stay up to date on extracurricular events and opportunities. We decided to design a mobile app that keeps students updated on the latest events and helps them stay involved in extracurriculars.

We used an iterative interaction design process to understand the users, design a solution, and iterate on the proposed design.

User Research

We conducted interviews and surveys to understand students needs when it comes to getting involved in extracurricular activities. We spoke to 5 students in various years with different interests.

We created personas, scenarios, and uses cases to summarize and categorize what we learned from other students. We then took these information to create requirements for the app design.

For my role, I led the user interviews, created personas, and drafted user requirements.

Design

Through our user research, we learned that students get involved in extracurriculars often by attending events. Furthermore, most students have specific preferences and interests. Thus, we thought the best way to help students get involved in extracurriculars is by recommending events and clubs based on their interests.

The app asks users for their areas of interest during the sign up process. The app makes recommendations bassed on users' interests. Users can register for events through the app. This will automatically save the event to their profile for later review.

We started with sketches to brainstorm various features and functionalities of the app. We created low-fidelity wireframes using Balsamiq and later high-fidelity prototype using Sketch and InVision.

Iteration

We conducted user testing, following the DECIDE framework, on the preliminary prototype to evaluate our app. We had 5 participants and each were asked to complete 3 tasks: sign up, register/unregister for an event, and follow/unfollow an event. Here are some of the major issues we identified as well as how we addressed them:

  • Users were confused by headers “Upcoming” and “Recommended”. We changed “Upcoming” to “Events” and “Recommended” to “Clubs”.
  • The layout of the “Recommended” or “Clubs” feed was confusing because it tried to highlight an upcoming event along with a club. The new "Club" feed only displays a list of recommended club.
  • Users wanted to know who else will be attending the events. We added public profiles to the app as well as a list of attendees in each event page.
  • It wasn't clear to users what "Home" meant. We changed it to "Discover" because the main concept behind the app is to effectively help users discover events and clubs.
