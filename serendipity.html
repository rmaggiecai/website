About the Project

Two friends and I got together for a reunion at YHack back in November 2014. We wanted to spend our 36 hours creating something unique, challenging, and fun. During our brainstorming session, we thought about all the problems we experienced in our everyday lives. We talked about typical problems, like Doodle never does what we want, and not so typical problems, like how to leverage a platform like Yo for marketing. They were all interesting problems, but none of them captured our hearts. Eventually, we agreed on a problem we all had during those grueling long road trips.

This past summer, my parents drove me from Toronto to New York City for my summer internship at MongoDB. The 8+ hour drive was long and boring. We always ate at McDonalds because they were the easiest thing to find. We drove past the Five Finger Lake without stopping because we had no idea how to get there. I thought, wouldn’t it be nice if we could explore during these long road trips without having to plan for them?

I wasn’t the only one with this problem. We all thought this would be an interesting problem to tackle, from a design perspective as well as from a technical perspective. We named the app: Serendipity.

Design

Because this was a hackathon and I was the only designer, I didn’t have the luxury to interview users or complete any of the HCI requirement gathering process. However, I was able to create scenarios to shape my ideas. I created sketches with these scenarios in mind, and then turned my sketches into high-fidelity mocks.

Scenarios

  • I am on a long drive back from a conference in Washington D.C. to New York City. I didn’t have time to plan, but I have some free time and would like to explore heritage towns along the way. I’m using Serendipity to help me find sightseeing locations and amazing gourmet restaurants.
  • I am new to Toronto and I rely on my GPS to get around. I am about to head to a restaurant on the other side of the city to meet a friend, but I am curious as to what are some interesting places along the way. I didn’t have time to do research, but I can use Serendipity to help me plan in just a few minutes.

High-fidelity Wireframes

With limited time, I chose to focus on:

  • Clear navigation instructions.
  • Easy-to-reach and easy-to-use buttons because users could be driving while using the app.

User Testing

After YHack, I decided to continue this as my side project. I did some user testing to review my designs.

I interviewed 3 participants who have all been on road trips before. I ask them to pretend that they are either driving or navigating for the driver while using the app. I asked the users to type in their destination, look for a restaurant along the route, and respond to a suggestion made by Serendipity. Below are the problems I found:

  • The meaning of distance and travel time for recommendations isn’t clear. Users are not sure if distance and travel time for recommendations are the distance and travel time from their current location or additional distance and travel time from their current route.
  • Users would like to have more information about the recommendations — such as hours of operation, phone number, and reviews — before making a decision.
  • Voice control, especially useful when the user is driving alone.
  • Users are not sure which dot indicates their current location and which dot indicates the destination.
